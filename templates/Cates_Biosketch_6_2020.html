Personal Statement

My research focus is to understand the neurological function which underlies human performance in order to develop novel interventions to improve both performance and recovery. My experience investigating neuromuscular adaptation and visual processing make me qualified to perform the proposed project on gaze behavior during locomotor learning. Vision is commonly known to be the dominant source of sensory information; however little research has examined how the use of visual information changes with motor learning, particularly during gait. While behavioral evidence suggests a difference, the underlying mechanisms of motor control are poorly understood. Before we can develop any intervention program, we must understand not only what is changing, but how. How do changes in Gaze behavior integrate with different motor control strategies and eventually motor performance? The proposed project is the first step in characterizing how gaze behavior changes during locomotor learning and would provide the necessary foundation to develop future interventions, should such a route prove valuable. My previous experiences have prepared me for such a course of action. Before starting my PhD at Northwestern, I completed undergraduate research with Dr. Ravi Thiruchselvam at Hamilton college, completing and undergraduate thesis on how individual mood impacts visual perception. I then conducted research at Halo Neuroscience on the mechanisms of motor learning and how transcranial direct current stimulation may accelerate or alter motor learning and neuromuscular control in athletic and clinical populations. Since starting my PhD at Northwestern University, I have investigated how training with visual perturbations may improve balance in a clinical population and developed pilot investigations to assess how gaze behavior changes during locomotor learning.
